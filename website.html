Concerning the art of diplomacy, a diplomat is defined as a person appointed by a state(monarchy/republic) or an intergovernmental institution (such as the United Nations or the European Union) to conduct diplomacy with one or more other states or international organizations. Statesmen: rubrik and discussions

Plato's Statesman: a premise for an ideal

The ideal statesman, as written by Plato in his socratic dialogue - The Statesman, otherwise known as Politicus in Latin, is conceptually a philosopher-king i.e. a true statesman must be a philsopher. According to Plato, a philosopher is someone who seeks wisdom and understanding of the world and is able to think critically and objectively. Thus, the statesman, as a philosopher, is able to use this wisdom and understandin to govern justly and in the people's interests. Additionally, Plato argued that the statesman must be able to discern different kinds of rulers - tyrants, demagogues and true statesmen, from one another. Tyrants and demagogues, Plato believed, were harmful to society as they pursued their selfish interests at the expense of the common good, whereas true statesmen were able to put the needs of the people above their own. Moreover, Plato stated that the statesman should also be knowledgeable about the arts and sciences, and should be able to apply this knowledge in the service of the state, arguing that statesmen should be well-rounded and well-educated in order to be able to make informed and wise decisions. Also, Plato believed in the concept of "the noble lie", which, he argued, was necessary in order to maintain social cohesion and stability. It was a belief that was held by the ruling class in order to maintain social harmony and to prevent social unrest.



Diplomats: Courtiers, Nobles and Executives of a Bureaucracy

Looking into diplomacy through the modern lens.

A person, who serves as a representative or an ambassador on behalf of the government of a particular country, may be referred to as a diplomat. (S)he plays a crucial role in maintaining healthy relations with other countries and their respective governments. A diplomat is, therefore, specifically skilled and trained individual who harbours the skills and tactfulness in dealing with matters concerning national and international politics which is setting of high stakes by default. A diplomat is appointed by the government is required to conduct conversations through diplomacy with representatives of other states or international organizations. (S)he is thus responsible for the maintainance of peaceful and amicable relations with other countries and may additionally be required to represent his/her nation's interests while promoting their nation in a positive light on the global stage. Oftentimes, they work in collaboration with another nation's foreign ministers thereby playing a key role in formulating a mutually beneficial foreign policy.

Notable Leaders

Some of the greatest leaders listed out as per their designation and achievements
  • Julius Caesar (12 July 100 BC – 15 March 44 BC)
    • Roman general and statesman
    • In 60 BC, Caesar, Crassus and Pompey formed the First Triumvirate, a political alliance that dominated Roman politics for several years.
    • Began a program of social and governmental reforms, including the creation of the Julian calendar.
    • Considered by many historians to be one of the greatest military commanders in history.

  • Alexander III of Macedon (20/21 July 356 BC – 10/11 June 323 BC)
    • Commonly known as Alexander the Great
    • King (basileus) of the ancient Greek kingdom of Macedon and a member of the Argead dynasty.
    • Spent most of his ruling years on an unprecedented military campaign through Asia and northeast Africa.
    • By the age of thirty he had created one of the largest empires of the ancient world, stretching from Greece to northwestern India.
    • Undefeated in battle and is widely considered one of history's most successful military commanders
    • Tutored by Aristotle until age 16.
    • Awarded the generalship of Greece and used this authority to launch his father's pan-Hellenic project to lead the Greeks in the conquest of Persia.
    • 334 BC, he invaded the Achaemenid Empire (Persian Empire) and began a series of campaigns that lasted 10 years.
    • Following the conquest of Anatolia, Alexander broke the power of Persia in a series of decisive battles, most notably the battles of Issus and Gaugamela.
    • invaded India in 326 BC, winning an important victory over the Pauravas at the Battle of the Hydaspes.

  • Socrates (c. 470 – 399 BC)
    • Classical Greek (Athenian) philosopher credited as one of the founders of Western philosophy
    • Made no writings, and is known chiefly through the accounts of classical writers writing after his lifetime, particularly his students Plato and Xenophon.
    • Renowned for his contributions to the fields of ethics and epistemology through Plato's dialogue.

  • Augustus Caesar (23 September 63 BC – 19 August AD 14)
    • Roman statesman and military leader who was the first emperor of the Roman Empire, reigning from 27 BC until his death.
    • His reign initiated an era of relative peace known as the Pax Romana.
    • Along with Mark Antony and Marcus Lepidus, he formed the Second Triumvirate to defeat the assassins of Caesar.
    • 31 BC: After the demise of the Second Triumvirate, Augustus restored the outward façade of the free Republic, with governmental power vested in the Roman Senate, the executive magistrates, and the legislative assemblies. In reality, however, he retained his autocratic power over the Republic as a military dictator.
    • Held a collection of powers granted to him for life by the Senate, including supreme military command, and those of tribune and censor. It took several years for Augustus to develop the framework within which a formally republican state could be led under his sole rule.
    • Rejected monarchical titles; instead called himself Princeps Civitatis ("First Citizen of the State").
    • Constitutional framework became known as the Principate, the first phase of the Roman Empire.
    • Dramatically enlarged the Empire, annexing Egypt, Dalmatia, Pannonia, Noricum, and Raetia, expanding possessions in Africa, and completing the conquest of Hispania; suffered a major setback in Germania.
    • Secured the Empire with a buffer region of client states and made peace with the Parthian Empire through diplomacy.
    • Reformed the Roman system of taxation, developed networks of roads with an official courier system, established a standing army, established the Praetorian Guard, created official police and fire-fighting services for Rome, and rebuilt much of the city during his reign.

  • Abraham Lincoln (February 12, 1809 – April 15, 1865)
    • American statesman and lawyer who served as the 16th president of the United States from 1861 until his assassination in April 1865
    • Led the nation through the American Civil War.
    • Preserved the Union, abolished slavery, strengthened the federal government, and modernized the U.S. economy
    • Self-educated, he became a lawyer, Whig Party leader, Illinois state legislator and Congressman.
    • Became a leader in the new Republican Party and gained national attention in 1858 for debating national Democratic leader Stephen A. Douglas in the 1858 Illinois Senate campaign. He then ran for President in 1860, sweeping the North and winning.

  • Nelson Mandela (18 July 1918 – 5 December 2013)
    • South African anti-apartheid revolutionary, political leader, and philanthropist who served as President of South Africa from 1994 to 1999
    • First black head of state and the first elected in a fully representative democratic election.
    • His government focused on dismantling the legacy of apartheid by tackling institutionalised racism and fostering racial reconciliation.
    • Served as President of the African National Congress (ANC) party from 1991 to 1997

  • Genghis Khan (born Temüjin, c. 1162 – August 18, 1227)
    • Founder and first Great Khan of the Mongol Empire.
    • Came to power by uniting many of the nomadic tribes of Northeast Asia.
    • Campaigns initiated in his lifetime include those against the Qara Khitai, Caucasus, and Khwarazmian, Western Xia and Jin dynasties.
